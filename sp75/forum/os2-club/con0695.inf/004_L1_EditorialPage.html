THE COMDEX EXPERIENCE
"Have you ever been experienced?
Well, I have."
- Jimi Hendrix

It's been three years since I was at Comdex. The last time was in Chicago where IBM was rolling out OS/2 version 2.0. I was attending as an exhibitor demonstrating our OS/2 based products. Now, I was returning as a member of the press corps, a new role for me, yet one I adapted to quickly. As a member of the press, I discovered all of the little perks that reporters and editors enjoy, such as: the press room, a media center where I could obtain all of the latest press releases, a break-out room with refreshments to relax and chat, and access to all of the presentations. It's interesting to see how attentive the exhibitors and presenters are when you're wearing a red press badge.

I'm always amazed at the scope of the computer industry and to see how it keeps growing and changing. Three years ago I was in awe of the scope of Comdex which filled up McCormick Place. I didn't think the show could get much bigger. Boy was I wrong ! This year's Comdex, held at the Georgia World Congress Center in Atlanta, dwarfed the Chicago conference and took up nearly every inch of the available 950,000 square feet. It was the largest Microsoft flea market I had ever seen.

Microsoft came out with its marketing guns blazing and used this Comdex to tout its Windows 95, the latest entry in the 32-bit operating systems wars. It was hard to miss a sign either saying "Building for Windows 95" or "Where do you want to go tomorrow?" Demonstrations of the beta version of Windows 95 were everywhere and I was surprised to see the public's reaction to it; somewhere between lukewarm and cold.

On the surface there have been many cosmetic changes made to Windows 95 to make it look like OS/2. But the similarities end there. I noticed the demonstrators were trained to tactfully dodge running multiple apps at the same time, in fear that multitasking might lock-up the computer. There were several other things that made me suspicious of the operating system; for example:

  • Files can now have names up to 244 characters long. Yet, when you check on the "properties" of the file, you see the archaic 8.3 naming convention still in place. To illustrate, suppose you name a file, "This is my file for this and that"; the operating system takes the first few letters of the file name and adds a numeric to uniquely identify it. In reality, the file's name is "This_i01.txt". This means the clunky Windows FAT file management system is still used in Windows 95.

  • Windows 95 claims to be "object oriented" yet you cannot make shadows of objects as you can under OS/2, nor search for the parents or children of files. Instead, files are found based on the closest fit to the file name. "Huh? You mean if I go looking for my file by its exact name, I might not find it?" That's right. The problem stems from Microsoft's desperate attempts to make Windows 95 behave like an object oriented operating system using the old underlying DOS 16-bit code.

  • When you try to call for a DOS window, the window refuses to open and you get a cryptic message saying "Incorrect DOS Version."

There are many other areas where people have pointed out deficiencies in Win95. The point is, it is much too easy to pick Windows 95 apart. As this publication goes to press, Microsoft is perilously close to the 60 day manufacturing period needed for its August delivery commitment. It is difficult to imagine that Microsoft will have all of the kinks worked out of the operating system in time for August delivery. This could spell real trouble for Microsoft if they lose the marketing momentum they've built up.

In a way, Windows 95 reminds me of the "new Coke" introduced in the mid-1980's which nearly toppled giant Coca-Cola. Like Coke, I believe Microsoft is grossly underestimating the public's reaction to its product. When the operating system sputters and coughs up blood, Microsoft is going to lose the allegiances of its loyal users. This is when IBM must strike.

For the last couple of years IBM has been saying "here is gold," yet the industry was content to settle for lead. While OS/2 was winning technical accolades, Microsoft was winning the hearts and minds of the public. Essentially, IBM has been trying unsuccessfully to slow down Microsoft's marketing machine. Now, it appears Microsoft is going to derail itself. In fact, if IBM is smart they could expedite the derailment by buying several copies of Windows 95 and simply display it next to OS/2. Even the most naive DOS/Windows user will quickly see the strengths of OS/2 and the flaws in Win95 in a side-by-side comparison.

Some people see Win95 as a ploy by Microsoft to stem the movement to OS/2 and to buy the company sufficient time to develop Cairo for Windows NT. Whether this is true or not is irrelevant. It's show time and Microsoft has stalled long enough. They simply have to deliver something, anything, to stop OS/2.

While the hubbub of Comdex was concentrated on "Windows 95", IBM's John Soyring quietly announced that OS/2 sales were accelerating and had surpassed 9 million copies sold. Perhaps IBM's latest marketing phrase sums up the state of OS/2. Aimed at lampooning Microsoft's "Where do you want to go tomorrow" phrase, IBM now touts OS/2 as "Where I want to go today" (see our bitmap of the month). - Tim Bryce Editor, OS/2 CONNECT
